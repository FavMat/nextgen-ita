The Chat Interface Was Just the Lobby

OpenClaw proved something the big labs spent hundreds of billions trying not to prove: for most real-world tasks, cheaper models running locally on a Mac Mini are good enough. The competitive moat was the model. Now it is not.

Six months ago, nobody had heard of OpenClaw. One Austrian developer built it alone. Last week, Jensen Huang dedicated part of his NVIDIA GTC keynote to it and called it "the most popular open-source project in the history of humanity."

That framing is worth pausing on. But it is not the actual story.

What the big labs were betting on

For years, OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and others raised hundreds of billions of dollars on a single foundational assumption: their models were untouchable. The gap between frontier and everything else was the competitive advantage. The moat was the capability delta. You could not replicate it without the compute, the data, the talent, the infrastructure.

OpenClaw cracked that bet. Not by building a better model. By proving that for most real-world tasks, cheaper models running locally on a Mac Mini are good enough. The cloud becomes optional. The premium becomes hard to justify.

The competition has shifted. It is not about who has the best model anymore. It is about who builds the best layer on top of it.

What actually differentiates now

If the model is no longer the moat, what is? The answer is the layer built on top of it. Agents and workflows: systems that actually do work rather than just answer questions. The distinction matters enormously in practice.

A chat interface gives you an answer. An agent completes a task. It reasons across steps, uses tools, maintains state, handles errors, and produces an output you can act on without translating it first. Most organizations are still at the chat interface stage. They are treating AI as a faster search engine.

The value is shifting, and it is shifting toward the people who figured out how to build on top of these models rather than the people who built the models themselves.

What this looks like in practice

I have been putting serious time into this for a while, building agents through Solaria (Deloitte's built-in GenAI platform) and through Claude and other models for my own work, things that genuinely changed how I run my day outside of professional projects too.

The pattern I keep seeing: the teams getting the most out of AI are not the ones with the best model access. They are the ones who redesigned their workflows around what agents can actually do, rather than grafting AI onto existing processes as a query tool.

The transition we are in is not from bad AI to good AI. It is from AI as a question-answering interface to AI as infrastructure that runs processes. Most people are still thinking in terms of the first paradigm.

OpenClaw is interesting because of what it demonstrates technically. But the real signal is what it points to: the lobby is crowded. The building is where the work actually happens, and most organizations have not walked through the door yet.
